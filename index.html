01 / Foundations

Product.

A monitoring surface for people whose job is to know what the conversation is, not to consume it. Bilingual, RTL-first, daily cadence, deliberately narrow in scope.

What problem the product solves

The product gives a small group of practitioners a daily, structured read of what is being said in Palestinian and Israeli social media and news about the Palestine/Israel situation. It collapses a high-volume, mostly Arabic, mostly Telegram (with Facebook and Twitter mixed in) stream into a per-day set of named topics with engagement, sentiment, key actors, viral posts, and source breakdowns — so a working analyst can see what changed today without scrolling raw feeds.

It is not a news reader. It is a monitoring surface for people whose job is to know what the conversation is, not to consume it.

Who the user is

A bilingual (Arabic-primary, English-comfortable) analyst, researcher, or journalist whose work involves tracking Palestinian and Israeli media narratives day by day. They have a recurring information need (daily or weekly), not a one-off curiosity. Technically literate enough to understand they are reading the output of an NLP pipeline rather than editorial judgement. They know the domain well enough to recognize when an entity name is garbled or a topic is mis-classified — they want the system to be honest about its uncertainty rather than smoothing it over.

Daily and weekly workflow

Core daily

Today's picture

Open the app, land on Daily Reports, pick a date and category lane, scan topics (title, summary, sentiment, dominant platform, engagement, key actors, viral posts), drill into one for source breakdown, temporal pattern, related topics in the same day's reports.

Optionally: ask the AI assistant about a topic, run an X-monitor session against a keyword, open the Analytics Board for cross-topic statistics, or open the Posts Explorer to look at raw posts.

Core weekly

The aggregate

Receive (or generate) a weekly report covering the same monitoring surface, locale-filtered (PS / IL / INT). Read it in-app or export it.

Optionally collect findings into the Report Workbench (evidence basket) and assemble a draft for distribution.

Domain scope

Currently scoped to Palestine/Israel-focused media monitoring. The data-app upstream, the category taxonomy (PS_*, IL_*, INT_*), the language pair (Arabic + English), and the publisher mix all reflect that scope. Architecturally the system is not tied to that domain — the same shape of system would work for any geography/topic-pair — but everything currently operating in production is. Stated explicitly so a future change of scope is a deliberate decision and not drift.

What the product is NOT

Negative definitions are load-bearing here. Several past feature proposals — real-time alerting, sentiment-comparison features, AI narrative synthesis — were declined because they would have implicitly made the product into one of the things on this list.

Not

A sentiment-analysis platform

Sentiment is one signal among several. The product surfaces it; it does not make sentiment-quality claims.

Not

A general-purpose social-listening tool

Curated corpus, domain-specific categories, NLP pipelines tuned for one domain.

Not

A news aggregator for end consumers

The audience is practitioners; surfaces show NLP-derived structure, not editorial digests.

Not

A real-time alerting system

Reports are produced on a daily cadence by the data app. There is no streaming alert path.

Not

A fact-checking product

The system shows what is being said, not whether it is true. Verification labels in the taxonomy reflect publisher mix, not adjudication.

Not

An LLM narrative synthesizer

The workbench presents what the user has collected. AI assistance there is for finishing/formatting, not for inventing entities or claims not in the source.

Seven product principles

Each principle is a direct application of the methodology in section 03. The connection is noted on each.

  1. Integrity over polish. No feature ships presenting confidence the data can't support. Applied: the ENABLE_ENTITY_FEATURES flag gating off synthesis and shared-entities; rejecting bare percentage labels with no measurement basis; rejecting LLM-generated narrative that introduced entities not in source material. Methodology link: confidence-tier discipline (§5).
  2. Honest labeling. Every claim the UI makes must be verifiable in the data. The bilingual error fields (error / errorAr) and the "no shared sources" affordance on topic-connections are small expressions of the same rule. Methodology link: descriptive vs inferential distinction (§8).
  3. Client-side only, for client-app features. The data app is not a dumping ground for new feature complexity. If a client-side feature would require data-app changes, that decision is escalated separately and tracked in the data app's own queue.
  4. Bilingual parity. Arabic and English receive equal attention; RTL/LTR is handled at layout level, not patched per-surface. Methodology link: NLP/language bias mitigation (§3 Bias 7).
  5. Same-day product scope (Topic Connections specifically). Topic-connections is within-report, same-day. Cross-day linkage has been rejected on this basis; any future "linked across time" proposal is a redefinition of the surface, not an extension. Methodology link: temporal bias control (§3 Bias 3).
  6. Preserve the data plumbing when cutting a feature. When a feature is gated off because the data isn't ready, the underlying computation, types, and fields stay in place so reactivation is a one-line flip. This is in tension with "delete dead code" — when the deadness is upstream-conditional, we preserve.
  7. Surface degradation, don't paper over it. When a field is inconsistent (mixed casings, mixed languages, partial population), surface the uncertainty or hide the dependent feature; don't silently coerce values into a guessed canonical form. Methodology link: AAPOR transparency principle (§9).

Architecture & data flow.

Six components, one push and one pull path between data app and client, two database connection pools, three external services. The contractual boundary that "the client app does not add work to the data app" is the constraint that has shaped most recent work.

The six components

Upstream

Data app

FastAPI service, owned by a different team. Ingests social-media and news content, runs NLP pipelines (entity resolution, sentiment, keyword extraction, topic clustering, temporal/source aggregation), assembles per-day reports. The client never calls the underlying NLP — it consumes finished products.

Client

Backend (Express + TypeScript)

A thin REST layer under /api/. Handles auth (sessions, 2FA, API-key for data-app webhooks), persistence via Drizzle ORM, scheduled jobs (node-cron for daily report sync), and the AI assistant / X-monitor sessions.

Client

Frontend (React 18)

Vite + Wouter + TanStack Query. Bilingual, RTL-first, shadcn/ui + Tailwind, dark/light themed, mobile-responsive. Most derived state — overlap scoring, filtering, ranking, chart shaping — is computed here from raw fields.

Storage

PostgreSQL on Neon

System of record. 36 tables. Holds data-app payloads (daily_reports, topics, raw_posts, weekly_reports, social_media_posts legacy) plus all client-owned data. Two connection pools separate user-facing reads from heavy batch sync work.

External

AI services

xAI Grok and OpenAI, used only by the client app's AI assistant and X-monitor surfaces, and for some report-finishing in the workbench. The data app does not depend on these — they are client-side conveniences.

External

Email (Resend)

Transactional only — verification, weekly subscription delivery, OTP, security mail. A future marketing/CRM platform is contemplated for campaign mail; OTPs and security mail will not move off Resend regardless.

Three contractual boundaries

This is the constraint that has shaped most recent work and that must be respected by future feature planning.

Boundary 1

The client app does not add work to the data app. If a client-side feature would require a new field, a re-classification, a different aggregation, or any data-app change, that work is scoped and tracked separately, escalated to the data-app team, and not committed to as part of the client task.

Boundary 2

The client app is responsible for honesty about what the data supports. If a field is unreliable in production, the client must either degrade gracefully, surface the uncertainty, or hide the dependent feature behind a flag — never paper over the gap with synthesised output.

Boundary 3

The data app owns analytical claims; the client owns presentation and workflow. The client may filter, sort, intersect, and chart what the data app emits. It may not invent new analytical claims.

The science of social listening.

Theory, practice, and scientific grounding for the kind of work this product enables. Social media data is not a random sample of any population — it is a convenience sample of the vocal, connected, and platform-active subset of any given group. This section is the epistemic backbone of every claim the product surfaces: what social listening can capture reliably, what it cannot capture without correction, and the disciplines that keep findings honest. Source: SSA Methodology Framework v2.1 (2024).

§1 — The representability problem

The core question

Can social media listening reflect on-ground public perspectives? Conditionally yes, but never without explicit qualification. Social media data is not a random sample of the public. It is a convenience sample of the vocal, connected, and platform-active subset of any given population — and that subset differs systematically from the general public in measurable ways.

Key structural facts

DimensionFigureImplication
Global social media penetration~62% (2024)38% of the world is structurally absent from social data
Active vs. passive user ratio~9:1 (lurker rule)91% of registered users never post publicly
Demographic overrepresentationYounger, urban, educated, digitally literateOlder, rural, lower-income populations systematically underrepresented
Platform self-selectionUsers choose platforms by identity and valuesPlatform choice is itself a data point, not neutral

What social listening is and is not

Social listening is a form of non-probability sampling of public digital expression. It cannot be treated as equivalent to a probability-based survey or poll. However, it generates valuable and valid data when the scope of claims is kept within its descriptive power.

Reliably captures

What social listening does well

  • The shape, volume, and trend direction of online conversation
  • Narrative frames and language patterns in use around a topic
  • Relative salience of issues and themes within a defined platform population
  • Speed and spread of emerging topics
  • Network structure of who is speaking to whom
Cannot reliably capture

Without explicit correction

  • Point-in-time prevalence of an opinion in the general population
  • The views of non-posting users (the silent majority)
  • Demographic-accurate sentiment splits
  • Predictions of offline behavior
  • Ground-truth public opinion

§2 — The validity gap

Two types of validity

The fundamental epistemological challenge of social media research is the distinction between two types of validity.

Ecological

How accurately the data reflects real-world cultural dynamics

Agenda-setting and narrative formation within online communities. Social listening has high ecological validity. It accurately describes what is being said, by whom, where, and when — within the captured population.

External

How accurately findings generalize

From the study population (social media users who post) to the target population of interest (the general public, all consumers, all citizens). Social listening has limited external validity by default, and any inferential claim requires explicit qualification and ideally cross-validation.

The validity spectrum

Claims made from social listening data exist on a spectrum from high to low validity:

Validity levelClaim typeExample
High — reliableIssue salience and volume"Mentions of [topic] increased 340% over Q3"
High — reliableNarrative and framing"The dominant frame is economic, not political"
High — reliableTrend direction"Negative sentiment has been declining consistently for 6 weeks"
Medium — qualifiedSentiment direction"The online conversation skews negative on this issue"
Low — needs cross-validationDemographic sentiment"Women aged 18–34 express more negative sentiment"
Very low — explicit caveatPopulation prevalence"63% of the public opposes this policy"

The academic consensus

The peer-reviewed literature is consistent and clear on this point:

Olteanu et al. (2019) identified five classes of data bias in social media research, concluding that "social media data is neither a representative nor a random sample of the population at large."

Hargittai (2020) in Whose Voices Are Represented in Social Science Research? demonstrated that digital trace data systematically overrepresents younger, urban, educated, and politically engaged populations — and that this skew varies significantly by platform.

The implication for practice: social data has high value as a cultural lens and limited value as a population survey — unless corrected through weighting, triangulation, or post-stratification.

§3 — Seven structural biases

Every piece of social media data carries structural biases that must be identified and disclosed. Each engagement runs through this taxonomy. Cards below give the mechanism, the direction the bias pulls findings, and the available mitigation.

Bias 1

Population bias

Mechanism

The population of social media users differs systematically from the general population. Platform users skew younger, more urban, more educated, and more digitally literate. Elderly, rural, and lower-income populations are structurally underrepresented.

Direction of distortion

Findings will overrepresent the perspectives of younger, urban, educated users. Sentiment from older demographics, rural communities, or lower-income groups is structurally absent.

Mitigation

Demographic reweighting where user-level data permits; triangulation with probability-based polls; explicit disclosure in methodology note.

Bias 2

Self-selection bias

Mechanism

Only motivated, emotionally engaged users post publicly. The vast majority — the 91% who register but never post — are invisible in social data. Those who do post are disproportionately driven by strong positive or negative emotion.

Direction of distortion

Sentiment polarity is inflated. The moderate, satisfied, or indifferent majority is structurally silent. Social data tends to make opinions look more polarized than they actually are in the full population.

Mitigation

Analyze the shape of sentiment distribution (bimodal vs. unimodal), not just the mean sentiment score. Flag when polarity skew is unusually high. Never extrapolate polarity figures to general population without cross-validation.

Bias 3

Temporal bias

Mechanism

Conversation volume spikes dramatically around news events, controversies, and viral moments. These spikes can represent 60–90% of a month's data, drowning out the organic, steady-state conversation that existed before and after the event.

Direction of distortion

Event-driven data distorts the baseline. A brand crisis, a celebrity mention, or a political moment can make it appear that public sentiment has shifted dramatically, when what has actually shifted is the volume of expressive attention — not the underlying opinion.

Mitigation

Rolling baseline windows (28-day smoothed trend); event normalization windows; isolation of event-driven spikes in reporting. Clearly distinguish event-driven from organic data periods.

Bias 4

Platform bias

Mechanism

Each platform has distinct cultural norms, demographic compositions, and discourse styles. These are not interchangeable lenses on the same public conversation — they are fundamentally different ecosystems.

PlatformCultural normDiscourse style
X (Twitter)Real-time reactionOutrage, political, breaking news
LinkedInProfessional identityOptimism, career, B2B
TikTokEntertainment and identityYouth culture, trends, humor
RedditEnthusiast communitiesIn-depth discussion, niche expertise
FacebookSocial connectionPersonal, community, local
InstagramAspiration and lifestyleVisual, brand-positive, emotional
Direction of distortion

Platform choice determines the cultural window through which a topic is viewed. Monitoring only X will produce a different — and more negative and polarized — picture than monitoring Facebook.

Mitigation

Multi-platform monitoring with documented platform weights; platform-stratified reporting; avoid single-platform findings being presented as general public opinion. Direct relevance: see Posts Explorer — current ingestion is Telegram-only.

Bias 5

Algorithmic amplification bias

Mechanism

Social media algorithms surface emotionally engaging, outrage-inducing, and shareable content. A post with 500 original authors but 5 million impressions has been algorithmically amplified — it does not represent 5 million independent opinion holders.

Direction of distortion

The reach of content is systematically skewed toward negative, extreme, and emotionally provocative content. Volume (number of posts) and reach (number of impressions) tell different stories, and conflating them produces a more negative and more extreme picture than reality.

Mitigation

Report volume (post count) and reach (impressions/engagements) as separate metrics. Clarify which figure is being used for sentiment calculations. Note high amplification ratios when present.

Bias 6

Content moderation bias

Mechanism

Platform moderation removes posts, suspends accounts, and — through shadow-banning — reduces the visibility of certain content before it is ever collected. The researcher's dataset is therefore not a sample of all expressed opinion, but of all expressed opinion that survived moderation.

Direction of distortion

Certain viewpoint categories — particularly extreme positions, conspiracy content, and coordinated inauthentic behavior — are partially censored before data collection begins. The direction of distortion depends on the platform's moderation philosophy.

Mitigation

Acknowledge in the limitations section that collected data reflects post-moderation expression. For topics where moderation is likely to be active (misinformation, extremism, coordinated campaigns), cross-check with lightly moderated platforms when relevant.

Bias 7

NLP & language bias

Mechanism

Most commercial sentiment analysis models were trained predominantly on English-language, Western-cultural text. Performance degrades significantly on: non-English languages, dialectal variation within a language, code-switching (mixing languages within a post), sarcasm, irony, cultural humor, and informal registers.

Direction of distortion

Systematic misclassification of non-standard or non-English content. For Arabic-language social data specifically: Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) vs. Egyptian, Levantine, and Gulf dialects are processed very differently by most commercial models, with dialectal Arabic producing significantly higher misclassification rates.

Mitigation

Use language-specific NLP models where available (CAMeL-Tools for Arabic, dialect-aware BERT models); conduct human validation on a minimum 5% stratified random sample (10% minimum for Arabic-primary reports); document model name and version in every report; flag known coverage limitations for non-English content. Direct relevance: see Integrity — sentiment_label mixed Arabic/English casing produces this exact failure mode in production.

§4 — Polling theory applied

The theoretical relationship

Classical probability-based polling and social media listening are complementary methodologies, not competitors. Understanding what polling theory offers — and where it fails to transfer — allows the practice to be positioned correctly and more rigorous studies to be designed.

Principles that transfer from polling

1. Define your target population explicitly

In traditional polling, the target population is specified precisely: "registered voters in the UK," "adults aged 18+ in Egypt," "users of [brand] product category." This same discipline must apply to social listening.

Application: Every report must define the social listening population — which platforms, which languages, which geographic filters, which account types (individual users only vs. including media/brand accounts), and which date window.

2. Establish and document your sampling frame

In polling, the sampling frame is the list from which respondents are drawn — voter rolls, phone directories, opt-in panels. In social listening, the sampling frame is defined by the keyword query set: the Boolean queries, hashtags, account handles, and URL patterns that determine which posts are collected.

An undocumented query is an undocumented sampling frame — an invisible source of sampling bias. Queries must be version-controlled and documented.

3. Distinguish descriptive from inferential claims

Polling generates inferential statistics — findings that, by virtue of random sampling, can be generalized to the target population within a calculable margin of error.

Social listening generates descriptive statistics — findings that accurately describe the captured dataset, which is not a random sample of any target population.

These two types of claims must never be conflated in client reporting without explicit correction language.

4. Apply post-collection weighting

Pollsters weight raw survey data by demographic variables (age, gender, region, education) to match known census distributions. This corrects for differential non-response and overrepresentation.

Social listening data can be subjected to analogous weighting — by platform share, language share, account type distribution, or geographic distribution — to improve alignment with the target population. The challenge is that reliable census benchmarks for social media populations are limited and platform-dependent.

§5 — Error margins & statistical theory

The margin of error problem

Unlike probability-based polling — where the margin of error is a mathematically precise function of sample size and sampling design — social listening has no universally accepted margin of error standard. This is a fundamental limitation acknowledged in the academic literature and should be disclosed in all client work. There are two distinct components to uncertainty in social listening data:

  • Statistical error (calculable) — the variance attributable to sample size, assuming the sample were representative.
  • Systematic bias error (non-calculable with precision) — the additional uncertainty introduced by all seven structural biases above. This cannot be reduced to a single number; it must be disclosed qualitatively.

The statistical error floor

Even though social data is not a probability sample, the classical margin of error formula can be applied to calculate the minimum statistical uncertainty — the floor below which confidence cannot go, even in the best case:

MoEstat = ±Z × √( p̂(1 − p̂) / n )

Where Z = Z-score for desired confidence level (1.645 for 90%, 1.96 for 95%, 2.576 for 99%), p̂ = observed proportion (e.g. 0.63 for 63% negative sentiment), n = number of posts/mentions analyzed. This formula gives the statistical lower bound on uncertainty only. It assumes the sample is representative, which social data is not. The true uncertainty is always greater than this figure.

Worked example

n = 2,000 posts p̂ = 0.63 (63% negative sentiment) Z = 1.96 (95% confidence) MoE = ±1.96 × √(0.63 × 0.37 / 2,000) = ±1.96 × √(0.0001166) = ±1.96 × 0.0108 = ±2.1% 95% CI: 60.9% to 65.1%

Even under optimal conditions, there is a ±2.1% statistical uncertainty in this proportion. With real-world systematic biases, the true uncertainty is considerably wider.

Confidence tiers based on evidence quality

In the absence of a universal MoE standard, the qualitative confidence tier system below covers evidence quality factors:

TierConditionsAppropriate claim strength
HighMulti-platform (3+), n ≥ 10,000, stable over 30+ days, cross-validated with survey/poll"Strong and consistent signal across platforms"
Moderate2–3 platforms, n = 1,000–9,999, 14–30 day window, no cross-validation"Data indicates a probable trend; validation recommended"
LowSingle platform, n < 1,000, event-driven spike, no validation"Early signal observed; interpret with caution"

The NLP accuracy dimension

Confidence in sentiment findings is also bounded by the accuracy of the NLP model used for classification. Most commercial sentiment models achieve:

Content typeClassification accuracy
English general text85–92%
MSA Arabic75–85% (model-dependent)
Dialectal Arabic60–75% (significant variation by dialect)

These rates translate directly into additional uncertainty on top of sampling uncertainty. A 63% negative sentiment finding from a model with 80% accuracy may reflect anywhere from 55% to 71% true negative sentiment.

§6 — Scientific sampling methods

Four methodologically grounded approaches exist for improving the representativeness and validity of social listening data.

Method 1 — Stratified Purposive Sampling Hsieh & Shannon (2005); Sloan & Morgan (2015)
Theory

Rather than collecting all available data from a keyword stream (which allows dominant subpopulations to define the sample), divide the potential data into strata defined by meaningful variables, then sample from each stratum proportionally.

Social media application

Define strata by: platform × language × account type (individual / media / brand) × region. Estimate the proportional contribution of each stratum to the target population. Weight the sample accordingly.

Strengths

Reduces dominance by any single demographic, platform, or subgroup. Allows subgroup-level analysis with adequate per-stratum sample sizes. Conceptually aligned with classical stratified random sampling.

Limitations

Requires estimating population stratum proportions, which are not always available for social media populations. Stratum-level data collection may require platform API access beyond standard listening tool capabilities.

Best for

Multi-platform reports with defined geographic scope; clients requiring subgroup analysis by region or language.

Method 2 — Temporal Random Sampling Standard epidemiological methodology adapted for streams
Theory

Rather than capturing the entirety of a keyword stream over a time period (which gives disproportionate weight to high-volume event periods), randomly select time windows for data collection. This distributes sampling evenly across time, reducing event-spike dominance.

Social media application

Define random sampling windows — e.g. 4 randomly selected 2-hour windows per day across 30 days — rather than continuous collection. Posts captured within these windows constitute the analytical sample.

Strengths

Reduces temporal bias and event-spike dominance. Computationally lighter than full-stream capture. Produces better baseline estimates for longitudinal tracking.

Limitations

May miss important event-driven conversation if the random window does not coincide with the event. Best combined with a separate event-capture collection when monitoring high-salience topics.

Best for

Always-on brand monitoring; longitudinal sentiment tracking; baseline measurement studies.

Method 3 — Triangulation Denzin (1978); Pew, YouGov, Reuters/Ipsos hybrid studies
Theory

No single methodology can provide complete, unbiased insight into a phenomenon. Triangulating across multiple independent data sources allows the strengths of one to compensate for the weaknesses of another. Where sources agree, confidence is elevated. Where they diverge, the divergence itself is informative.

Social media application

Combine social listening data with at least one probability-based source — a survey, a published poll, an official statistic, or a client's proprietary panel data.

  • Social listening provides: narrative texture, real-time speed, thematic depth, emerging topic identification, verbatim language analysis
  • Probability polling provides: population-representative prevalence estimates, demographic breakdowns, inferential validity
Strengths

The most scientifically defensible approach for making inferential claims about general public opinion from social data. Used by the world's leading research institutions. Significantly elevates the confidence tier of any finding where both sources agree.

Limitations

Requires access to a probability-based data source, which typically adds cost and time. Interpretation of divergence between sources requires methodological expertise.

Best for

Strategic client work; reports where population-level inferential claims are required; premium hybrid research products; reports that will inform significant business or policy decisions.

Method 4 — MRP (Multilevel Regression with Post-stratification) Gelman & Little (1997); Wang et al. (2015)
Theory

MRP is a two-stage statistical technique. In stage one, a multilevel regression model is built using demographic variables to predict opinion. In stage two, the model's predictions are weighted (post-stratified) to match the known demographic distribution of the target population from census data. This allows non-representative datasets to produce population-representative estimates.

The Wang et al. (2015) demonstration

Using data from 750,000 Xbox gaming platform users — a dataset radically unrepresentative of the US voting population (young, male, gamer-skewing) — Wang et al. applied MRP to accurately forecast the 2012 US presidential election outcome. The study demonstrated that even severely biased convenience samples can produce reliable population estimates when sufficient demographic information is available for reweighting.

Social media application

Infer user demographics from social media signals (language, location, account characteristics, behavioral patterns). Build a multilevel regression model predicting sentiment or opinion from demographic variables. Post-stratify against census or known population distributions.

Strengths

The theoretically most powerful approach for converting social data into population-representative estimates. Enables inferential claims with documented statistical uncertainty. Increasingly used in academic computational social science.

Limitations

Requires sufficient demographic signal in the dataset for reliable inference. Computationally and statistically intensive. Requires specialized expertise to execute correctly. Quality is bounded by the accuracy of demographic inference from social signals.

Best for

Research-grade deliverables; government and academic partnerships; high-budget campaigns where population-level inferential claims are the central deliverable.

§7 — The four-tier confidence label system

Every quantitative finding in a research-grade report carries one of four standardized confidence labels. The product's velocity badges and momentum classifications follow this same calibration discipline.

🟢 Signal Confirmed

Supported by multiple independent data sources; meets all criteria for high-confidence designation.

Criteria (all must be met)
  • Monitoring across 3 or more platforms
  • Total n ≥ 10,000 posts
  • Trend stable across 30+ consecutive days
  • Cross-validated with at least one probability-based source
Report language

"Strong and consistent signal across platforms, corroborated by [source]. The finding is robust to platform-level variation."

Appropriate for

Executive summary claims; strategic recommendations; findings informing significant business decisions.

🔵 Signal Detected

Consistent across the available data but not cross-validated against an external probability-based source.

Criteria
  • 2 or more platforms
  • n = 1,000 to 9,999 posts
  • Observed across a 14 to 30-day window
  • No external cross-validation available
Report language

"Data indicates a probable trend. Further validation against survey or polling data is recommended before drawing population-level conclusions."

Appropriate for

Most standard client reporting; operational and tactical findings; directional guidance.

🟡 Signal Emerging

The pattern is visible in the data but the evidence base is too sparse, too recent, or too event-driven to be confident of its durability or representativeness.

Criteria
  • Single platform, or sparse data across platforms
  • n < 1,000 posts
  • Observed over less than 14 days
  • High probability of event-driven distortion
Report language

"Early signal observed. Interpret with caution — the data window is short and the sample is limited. Monitor for development over the next 2–4 weeks."

Appropriate for

Early-warning monitoring alerts; trend-watch outputs; flagging topics for deeper investigation.

🔴 Artifact — Verify

The pattern detected is anomalous and may reflect data quality issues rather than genuine sentiment or opinion.

Triggers
  • Unusual spike inconsistent with preceding trend
  • Suspected bot or coordinated inauthentic behavior
  • High NLP model flag rate or known misclassification patterns
  • Single-source domination (one account or one publisher)
  • Data collection or query anomaly
Report language

"Anomalous pattern detected. Manual review recommended before this finding is acted upon. Possible explanations include [bot activity / event spike / NLP error / data collection issue]."

Appropriate for

Flagging in monitoring dashboards; bringing to client attention before final delivery; initiating investigation.

Confidence label decision tree (Appendix C)
Is the finding cross-validated with a probability-based source? ├── YES → Are 3+ platforms represented with n ≥ 10,000 and 30+ days? │ ├── YES → 🟢 SIGNAL CONFIRMED │ └── NO → 🔵 SIGNAL DETECTED (with cross-validation noted) └── NO → Is n ≥ 1,000 across 2+ platforms over 14+ days? ├── YES → 🔵 SIGNAL DETECTED └── NO → Are there anomalies (spikes, bots, NLP flags)? ├── YES → 🔴 ARTIFACT — VERIFY └── NO → 🟡 SIGNAL EMERGING
What this means in this product

The four-tier system above is the SSA house standard. The product does not currently implement it. The fields the product surfaces — topics.velocity_status, topics.momentum, topics.trend_direction, topics.velocity_score — encode directional movement (rising / stable / declining), not evidence quality (n / platform-breadth / temporal stability / cross-validation). They are not a mapping to Signal Confirmed / Detected / Emerging / Artifact–Verify, and they should not be presented to users as if they were. Adopting the four-tier system would require:

  • A per-topic n_posts measure that the UI can read alongside the existing fields (already present as cluster_size in unified_topics; not yet surfaced on Daily Reports).
  • A platform-breadth count (currently degraded by the Telegram-only raw_posts reality — see Posts Explorer).
  • A temporal-stability check across consecutive days (Pipeline C's cross-day continuity surface — topic_stable_id, is_carried_forward, prior_topic_post_count — supplies the underpinnings, but the four-tier classifier does not yet exist).
  • A cross-validation gate against an external probability-based source (no infrastructure exists for this).

The off-limits list in Open Questions includes "the integrity-over-polish data principle"; the corollary is that adopting the four-tier system is a real product decision, not a labeling change. It is in scope for a future Phase 2 conversation, not for incremental UI work.

§9 — AAPOR transparency framework

The American Association for Public Opinion Research (AAPOR) transparency standards represent the closest formally endorsed framework applicable to social listening data disclosure. AAPOR has extended guidance to non-probability and digital data sources.

Six core principles (adapted for social data)

1

Population definition

Who is the population of interest? What is the social media population being monitored? Be explicit about inclusions and exclusions.

2

Sampling and data collection

How was data collected? Which platforms, APIs, and collection methods were used? What were the query parameters?

3

Sample size and design

How many posts were analyzed? Over what time period? What stratification or sampling strategy was applied?

4

Non-response and coverage

What is not captured? What are the known gaps in coverage? For social listening: what % of the true conversation does the query likely capture?

5

Questionnaire / instrument

For social listening: the keyword query set is the instrument. It must be documented and disclosed.

6

Data processing

What NLP model was used? What pre-processing was applied? What was the human validation rate?

Reports that aspire to research-grade status should be fully disclosable under AAPOR standards: if a client or regulator asked for the complete methodology documentation, it could be provided. Methodological opacity is both a scientific weakness and a commercial risk.

§8 — Methodology implications

The watchlist as sampling frame

From methodology §4: "In polling, the sampling frame is the list from which respondents are drawn. In social listening, the sampling frame is defined by the keyword query set and account list." The de-facto watchlist documented above is the system's sampling frame. Two consequences:

  • Any descriptive claim about "what is being said in Palestinian media" is precise within the monitored set. Generalizing to all Palestinian media discourse requires the population assumption that the monitored set is representative of the whole — which is not currently verified.
  • Changes to the watchlist (additions, removals, account suspensions) shift the sampling frame. Without a documented account list, frame-shifts are not auditable after the fact. A spike or drop in coverage on some topic cannot be cleanly attributed to "the world changed" vs. "the monitored accounts changed."

What honest reporting would require

Per methodology §9 AAPOR Principle 5 (questionnaire / instrument):

  • The monitored-account list should be versioned — every add/remove timestamped.
  • Reports should carry the account-list version they were generated against (akin to query_version in the AAPOR-aligned appendix).
  • Longitudinal claims should be checked against frame-stability between the periods compared.

None of this is currently implemented at the client side. The data-app side may have the versioning primitives; the client does not surface them.

05 / The Two Apps

Data app.

A separate FastAPI service, owned by another team. Does the only thing the client cannot do for itself: turns raw upstream content into a per-day, per-category envelope of named topics with NLP signals attached. The client sees its outputs, never its internals.

What the data app does that wouldn't otherwise happen

  • Ingests raw upstream content. Telegram for sure; other platforms apparently — since topics.viral_posts carries Facebook and Twitter posts that never reach raw_posts (data-inventory finding #7).
  • Runs the NLP pipelines: sentiment labelling, keyword extraction, geo-location extraction, entity resolution into actors_structured, topic clustering, dashboard-section classification, copy-vs-original detection on raw posts, and per-topic temporal/source/platform aggregation.
  • Assembles the daily-report payload — a per-day, per-category envelope containing the day's topics with all the aggregated signals.

Without the data app, the client has nothing to render. There is no fallback ingestion path; the data app is the upstream.

§5 — Authentication

Both portals use the same auth implemented in auth.py.

5.1 · Login flow

Endpoint: POST /api/auth/login with email + password. The email must end in @socialstudio.me (auth.ALLOWED_DOMAIN); requests from other domains are rejected with 403.

5.2 · Token

On success the server issues a JWT (HS256, 24-hour expiry) signed with the secret loaded from the JWT_SECRET environment variable. The actual secret value is never committed to the repo and never logged. If JWT_SECRET is unset the process fails to start with a clear error.

5.3 · Storage

The JWT is set as a httponly samesite=lax cookie named admin_token and returned in the JSON response so SPA / API clients can also use a Bearer header.

5.4 · Roles

viewer < admin < superadmin (see ROLE_RANK in api.py). Most endpoints require admin; user-management endpoints require superadmin.

5.5 · Rate limiting

5 failed login attempts per IP per 5 minutes (auth.RATE_LIMIT_MAX_ATTEMPTS / RATE_LIMIT_WINDOW_SECONDS). Successful logins reset the counter.

5.6 · Lockout recovery

Wait out the rate-limit window or restart the server to clear the in-memory rate-limit store. If an operator has forgotten their password, a superadmin can reset it through the admin-users management endpoints; see the audit trail in admin_audit_log. The exact recovery procedure intentionally lives outside this repo.

3.1 Trigger schedule — continuous poll, not cron High confidence

Pipeline C does not use a cron expression. It uses a continuous poll loop with a wall-clock floor:

3.2 Retry policy when posts.csv is not yet in S3 High confidence · L305-355

§5 — Topic cap and selection metrics

5.1 No per-day topic cap exists High confidence — intentional per project context

There is no per-day topic cap in Pipeline C. rg "MAX_TOPICS|topic.*cap|max_topics|MAX_CLUSTERS" pipeline_c_*.py returns zero hits. Every cluster the HDBSCAN gate emits is classified, persisted, and dispatched to Stage 7.

Bound on dispatch: pipeline_c_dispatch.DISPATCH_MAX_WORKERS = 6 is a concurrency bound, not a topic cap — it limits parallelism, not total count.

This is intentional per replit.md ("MAX_TOPICS=20 is segmented-only; Pipeline C has NO topic cap"). The 2026-05-07 scheduled run held 119 LLM-filled topics in a single run.

Risk

At ≥20 topics, per-topic LLM call cost (~8 model calls per topic, 2 languages × 4 sub-prompts) scales linearly with no operator guard rail. The webhook payload also balloons.

5.2 Cluster selection metrics — what gates publication High confidence
  • HDBSCAN density — min_cluster_size = 8 (env: PIPELINE_C_MIN_CLUSTER_SIZE) and min_samples = 3. Below these floors is HDBSCAN noise (label −1), filtered out before persistence.
  • Domain routing gate — anchor cosine similarity must clear LLM_FALLBACK_ABS_FLOOR=0.45 AND beat the runner-up by LLM_FALLBACK_DELTA=0.05. If neither, an LLM fallback classifier gets a 10-post sample (LLM_FALLBACK_SAMPLE_SIZE=10); if even the LLM returns junk, the cluster is labelled domain_primary='unclassified' and still persisted.
  • Cross-day continuity (informational, not gating) — cosine ≤ 0.30.

There is no minimum source-diversity check, minimum total engagement check, minimum narrative-coherence score, or minimum cross-source corroboration check. ADR 0006 mentions "Topic Cohesion Diagnostics" — these are computed and logged but do not gate publication.

5.3 No ranking-and-cap function High confidence on absence

There is no ranking-and-cap function because there is no cap. The Stage 7 dispatcher iterates the full list returned by db.get_unified_topics(report_id) and submits every one to ThreadPoolExecutor(max_workers=6).

Each cleaned post is transformed into a dense 768-dimensional numerical vector using the paraphrase-multilingual-mpnet-base-v2 model from the Sentence Transformers library. Stage 5 · UMAP Dimensionality Reduction Reduce

Raw 768-dimensional embeddings are far too high-dimensional for density-based clustering algorithms to work effectively (the curse of dimensionality). UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) is used to reduce the embeddings to a lower-dimensional space that preserves the global semantic structure while making clusters more compact and separable.

Two separate UMAP projections are computed for different purposes:

  • 30-dimensional UMAP (clustering): Used as input to HDBSCAN. A high n_neighbors value (50) ensures the global topic structure is captured. min_dist=0.0 creates tightly packed clusters, which is ideal for HDBSCAN's density estimation.
  • 2-dimensional UMAP (visualisation): Used only to generate scatter-plot visualisations. min_dist=0.1 spreads points apart for readability. This projection is never used for clustering.

Stage 8 · Subtopic Clustering — Local UMAP + HDBSCAN ML

For each main topic with 50 or more posts, a dedicated local UMAP + HDBSCAN pipeline is run using only the embeddings belonging to that topic. This local approach reveals within-topic structure that is invisible in the global 30-dimensional space because the global projection compresses fine-grained distinctions.

Topics with fewer than 50 posts are assigned a single subtopic (ID = 1) and are not sub-clustered.

  • Local n_components is capped at min(10, topic_size - 2) for the same reason
  • sub_min_cluster_size is auto-derived as max(10, int(topic_size × 0.08))
  • sub_min_samples is auto-derived as max(3, int(sub_mcs × 0.40))

Stage 9 · Event Clustering — Agglomerative ML

Within each subtopic that has X or more posts (where X is unspecified in the source — see §5 below), Agglomerative Clustering groups posts into discrete events. Unlike HDBSCAN, agglomerative clustering produces no noise — every post is assigned to a group. However, groups smaller than MIN_EVENT_GROUP_SIZE (3 posts) are discarded and their posts are labelled -1 (event noise).

Agglomerative clustering with a cosine distance threshold of 0.25 and average linkage was chosen for this level because it reliably partitions dense subtopic clusters into coherent, actionable event groups without requiring a pre-specified number of clusters.

Event IDs follow the three-level dot notation: TOPIC.SUBTOPIC.EVENT (e.g., 2.1.3).

§6 — Why this is worth evaluating

Three reasons the prototype is interesting relative to the existing production stack, framed neutrally as evaluation criteria — not as a recommendation to adopt.

Hierarchy

Three-level structure

Pipeline C produces one level of topics. This prototype produces Topic → Subtopic → Event. If the additional granularity surfaces structure analysts currently can't see, it may justify a structurally larger output payload. If it produces splits that don't track real analyst intuition, it adds complexity without value. Evaluation question: do the Level 2 and Level 3 splits match the way analysts already mentally subdivide a topic?

Reproducibility

Deterministic by design

Pipeline C is stochastic in places. This prototype fixes random seeds at every stochastic stage — re-running on identical input produces identical output. For audit, regression testing, and "what changed since last run?" investigations, deterministic output is a measurable improvement.

Parameter discipline

Sweep-then-pick

Pipeline C uses fixed-constant clustering parameters. This prototype foregrounds parameter selection as an explicit sweep over 8 candidates with three diagnostic metrics. Aligns with the methodology's confidence-tier discipline (§5 and §7) — measure cluster quality before committing.

Reasons against: sample-based (3,000 posts is < 2% of a typical day's raw_posts volume); does not document a narrative-labelling layer; does not document cross-day continuity; lives outside both production repos so adopting it means writing new integration code. The decision is not made here.

Glossary & reference.

Disambiguating terms that appear across the documentation set, plus pointers to the canonical reference documents this site is derived from.

Glossary

Report (daily)
A per-day, per-category envelope produced by the data app. One day generates many reports (one per category; bilingual — language is ar or en, ~50/50). Stored in daily_reports.
Report (weekly)
A locale-filtered (PS / IL / INT) aggregation of a week's daily reports. Stored in weekly_reports.
Topic
The data app's unit of NLP-derived grouping within a daily report. Linked to one daily report, one category, one language. Lifespan: same as its report.
Source
Disambiguate by context. Publishing source — the upstream account/outlet. Data source — the data app, as the system that provides payloads. Signal source — the field a chart is reading from (e.g. topics.source_analytics_data).
Post (raw)
raw_posts — the live ingestion surface, currently Telegram-only, ~168k rows, with NLP fields. Backs Posts Explorer, Analytics Board, AI Assistant context.
Post (legacy)
social_media_posts — residue of two earlier pivots (CSV upload + in-process LLM, then per-report posts payload). Currently still receiving inserts despite Task #71's intent to stop them.
Viral post
A pre-baked, per-topic snippet (viral_posts jsonb on topics) chosen by the data app. The only place Facebook/Twitter content surfaces, since raw_posts is Telegram-only.
Distinctive keyword
In topic-connections, a keyword that appears in one topic but not the other being compared.
Watched keyword
A user-tracked term that drives filtering on Posts Explorer and influences report-generation context.
Evidence block
A typed unit collected into the workbench (ai_response, x_monitor_result, post, chart_snapshot, topic_discovery, narrative_text).
Category
A taxonomy slug like PS_Media, IL_Government, INT_Solidarity, plus legacy slugs that route through LEGACY_CATEGORY_MAP in shared/categories.ts. ~21.65% of topics use legacy slugs.
Dashboard section
A separate, looser taxonomy on raw_posts.dashboard_section. Mixes registry slugs with values not in the registry (News, Government) and a known typo (INT_Verificaiton).
Pipeline B
The legacy per-category processing pipeline. Writes to topics.
Pipeline C
The unified, all-source same-day pipeline. Writes to unified_topics with separate viewpoints_ar / viewpoints_en.
call_llm
The data app's single LLM entry point at processing.py:818. Routes by call_type through LLM_CALL_REGISTRY.
Preset
A named LLM configuration in seed_presets.py: Factual Reporter (default) · Media Monitor · Analyst · Political Analyst.
Methodology terms (added v2.0)
Methodology
The discipline of social-listening as a research practice — what it can capture reliably, what it cannot capture without correction, and how to communicate findings honestly. See section 03.
Ecological validity
How accurately data reflects real-world cultural dynamics, agenda-setting, and narrative formation within online communities. Social listening has high ecological validity. Methodology §2.
External validity
How accurately findings generalize from the study population (social media users who post) to the target population of interest (the general public). Social listening has limited external validity by default. Methodology §2.
Convenience sample
A non-random sample drawn from whatever is accessible. Social media data is by definition a convenience sample of the vocal, connected, platform-active subset of any group.
Lurker rule
The empirical observation that the active-vs-passive user ratio on social platforms is roughly 9:1 — about 91% of registered users never post publicly.
Population bias / Self-selection bias / Temporal bias / Platform bias / Algorithmic amplification bias / Content moderation bias / NLP & language bias
The seven structural biases inventoried in the methodology. Each must be identified and disclosed for every engagement. Methodology §3.
Triangulation
Combining social-listening data with at least one probability-based source (a survey, a published poll, an official statistic) to elevate confidence. Methodology §6 method 3; Denzin (1978).
MRP — Multilevel Regression with Post-stratification
A two-stage statistical technique for converting non-representative datasets into population-representative estimates by reweighting against known demographic distributions. Methodology §6 method 4; Gelman & Little (1997); Wang et al. (2015).
Stratified purposive sampling
Dividing the potential data into meaningful strata (platform × language × account type × region) and sampling proportionally rather than collecting all available data from a keyword stream. Methodology §6 method 1.
MoE — Margin of Error
For social listening, only the statistical floor is calculable (Z × √(p̂(1−p̂)/n)); the systematic-bias component cannot be reduced to a single number. Methodology §5.
AAPOR
American Association for Public Opinion Research. AAPOR's Transparency Initiative is the closest formally endorsed disclosure standard applicable to social-listening data. Methodology §9; AAPOR (2023).
Signal Confirmed / Signal Detected / Signal Emerging / Artifact–Verify
The four-tier confidence label system. Every quantitative finding in a research-grade report carries one of these labels. The product does not currently use this taxonomy — see the "What this means in this product" callout in methodology §7.
Descriptive vs inferential
The most important language rule: descriptive language for what the data shows; inferential language only when cross-validated. Methodology §8.
Olteanu et al. (2019) / Hargittai (2020) / Wang et al. (2015)
The foundational methodology references. Olteanu identified five classes of social-data bias; Hargittai demonstrated systematic demographic skew in digital trace data; Wang et al. (the "Xbox study") showed MRP can recover population estimates from severely biased samples. Methodology §10.
Jaccard similarity
Set-overlap measure: |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|. Used in the client-side Topic Connections panel as IDF-weighted Jaccard against keyword sets and (gated-off) actor sets.
HDBSCAN
Density-based clustering algorithm. Pipeline C's whole-day clustering uses HDBSCAN with min_cluster_size=8, min_samples=3 on L2-normalized embeddings.
IDF
Inverse Document Frequency. Weights keywords by rarity across the corpus; used in the topic-overlap scorer to downweight common terms.
ADR 0010
The architectural decision record formalizing the strict-coexistence rule that Pipeline C never mutates Pipeline B state. Cited inline in the dispatcher, orchestrator, and webhook hook.
Cohesion diagnostics
Per-topic computed metrics (cohesion score, single-author share, low-similarity posts) that Pipeline C records for every cluster but does not use to gate publication. Future "Phase 2" reject thresholds candidate.
